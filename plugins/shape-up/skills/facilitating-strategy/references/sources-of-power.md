# Sources of Power — Rumelt chs. 6–15

A labeled vocabulary for naming the user's intuitions in the moment. When the user says something sound and intuitive, match it to the right label, say it back, and hand them the deliberate handle. Each entry: the mechanism, the trigger (what the user says that signals it), and a book case.

This is the move "affirm the intuition before sharpening it." The user feels seen, then equipped. It requires you to actually know these, so scan for the match rather than reaching for whichever label is nearest.

---

## Insight / changing the frame (positioning)
**Mechanism:** Advantage from a fresh viewpoint that reveals hidden asymmetry — strength where others see weakness, or weakness inside apparent strength; or redefining the unit of competition.
**Trigger:** the user's manifesto, their "everyone assumes X but actually Y", the reframe that makes the idea feel strong. Usually this is positioning, **not** the diagnosis — name it as insight and then ask for the org's own challenge separately.
**Cases:** David vs. Goliath (the sling turns size into a liability). Wal-Mart vs. Kmart (the unit became a regional network around distribution, not a single store).

## Leverage (ch. 6)
**Mechanism:** anticipation + pivot point + concentration. Concentrate effort where a small push releases much larger forces.
**Trigger:** "this shift is coming and most people aren't acting on it yet"; a predictable future behavior of customers/rivals/institutions worth concentrating ahead of.
**Cases:** Toyota concentrating $1B on hybrids ahead of fuel-economy pressure. 7-Eleven Japan's pivot on local taste and freshness.

## Proximate objectives (ch. 7)
**Mechanism:** an objective close enough to be feasible. A leader absorbs ambiguity and hands the organization a problem it can actually solve.
**Trigger:** the objective is too distant or abstract to act on Monday morning; or the team is paralyzed by an ambiguity nobody will resolve.
**Cases:** Kennedy's moon landing (bold but proximate; engineers knew the steps). Phyllis Buwalda specifying "Phyllis's moon" so Surveyor could be designed before the science was settled.

## Chain-link systems (ch. 8)
**Mechanism:** performance is capped by the weakest link. Improving strong links does nothing while a weak link remains. Excellence that is chain-linked is hard to copy.
**Trigger:** "fixing only one of these won't help because they all depend on each other"; or the user wants to pour effort into something already strong.
**Cases:** Marco Tinelli's machinery firm (quality, then sales, then cost, in sequence). IKEA (a chain of mutually supporting policies a rival cannot copy one piece at a time).

## Design (ch. 9)
**Mechanism:** strategy as design, not selection from pre-given options — creating fit among actions, resources, constraints. Weaker resources demand tighter integration.
**Trigger:** the user is picking from generic best-practice options instead of designing a system whose parts fit; or has weak resources and needs the parts to compound.
**Cases:** Hannibal at Cannae (premeditated coordinated movement turns Roman numbers into compressed immobility). Paccar trucks (a designed system around the owner-driver's view of value).

## Focus (ch. 10)
**Mechanism:** concentrate distinctive policies onto a segment where those policies create disproportionate power. Both concentration of policies and concentration on a target.
**Trigger:** "we're trying to serve everyone"; or a set of policies that only pays off for one specific kind of customer/situation.
**Cases:** Crown Cork & Seal (everything tuned to short production runs and rush orders for smaller customers). Paccar (large-truck strategy built around owner-drivers).

## Growth (ch. 11)
**Mechanism:** healthy growth is the *outcome* of a successful strategy, not a strategy itself. Growth-by-acquisition usually destroys value unless you add value no other buyer can, or buy cheaply.
**Trigger:** the user lists growth ("20% a year", "expand to Europe", "acquire X") *as* the strategy.
**Cases:** Crown after Connelly (growth-by-acquisition left the focused advantage behind; stock fell from $55 to $5). Telecom Italia / Cable & Wireless (combining cash flows is arithmetic, not value creation).

## Advantage (ch. 12)
**Mechanism:** advantage is rooted in asymmetry, but owning it is not enough — wealth grows only when the advantage *increases* or demand for the underlying resource rises. Mechanisms: deepen, broaden, raise demand, strengthen isolation.
**Trigger:** the user has a real edge but it is static; or asks why a strong position is not creating value.
**Cases:** Resnick / Roll International in nuts (holdings large enough to make research, branding, processing worthwhile; the tree-maturation lag gives time to build advantage). eBay / the "silver machine" (a real advantage that cannot be increased creates no new wealth).

## Dynamics (ch. 13)
**Mechanism:** big opportunities open during waves of external change — technology, deregulation, cost shifts, buyer shifts. You need not forecast perfectly; you need to see the present trajectory (the "attractor state") more clearly than rivals.
**Trigger:** "the ground is shifting under this industry"; an incumbent blocked by its current profit stream or doctrine.
**Cases:** Cisco riding several networking waves. Newspapers being unbundled — the challenge was which functions to unbundle, not "move online" in the abstract.

## Inertia and entropy (ch. 14)
**Mechanism:** inertia is the inability or unwillingness to adapt; entropy is drift into disorder. Strategy can exploit rivals' inertia and must constantly fight its own entropy.
**Trigger:** "established users can't move — they have their habits / spreadsheets / installed base"; or internal clutter and cross-subsidy nobody is weeding. Attacking where there is no habit to overcome is attacking weak inertia.
**Cases:** Continental's regulated-era fleet planner misleading management after deregulation. Denton's garden outlets — weeding five weak ones raised GTO from ~$100k to >$5M (not innovation; weeding entropy).

## Putting it together — Nvidia (ch. 15)
**Mechanism:** the full kernel under coordination plus tempo. Nvidia removed dependencies to run a six-month graphics cycle against an eighteen-month industry cycle; rivals' own routines made the strategy hard to copy.
**Trigger:** the user's strategy depends on a tempo or coordination that requires removing or controlling specific dependencies; or works partly because rivals can't or won't match it.
**Use:** ask "what dependencies must be removed or controlled to make this tempo possible?" and "which rival routines make this hard to copy?"
