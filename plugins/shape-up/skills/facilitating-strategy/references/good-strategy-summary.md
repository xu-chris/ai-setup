# Good Strategy / Bad Strategy — quick-lookup

Condensed reference for mid-session consultation. Richard Rumelt, 2011. For the source-of-power vocabulary use `sources-of-power.md`; this file is the kernel, the hallmarks, and the cases that illustrate the core moves.

---

## The kernel

A good strategy has three parts. Missing one means it is not a strategy.

1. **Diagnosis** — explain what is going on; simplify by naming the one or two critical factors. Defines the field of action.
2. **Guiding policy** — the overall approach to the obstacle the diagnosis named. Rules alternatives out.
3. **Coherent action** — coordinated steps that carry out the policy and **reinforce one another**.

> "A great deal of strategy work is trying to figure out what is going on… not just deciding what to do, but the more fundamental problem of comprehending the situation."

**Coherence test:** do the actions explain why they belong together, or merely sit in a list? Do they reinforce, or compete for the same scarce resources?

**Cases:** Gerstner re-diagnosed IBM — not "too integrated," but failing to use its uncommon breadth; policy became cross-boundary customer solutions. Stephanie's grocery — diagnosed competition with a 24/7 supermarket, chose the time-pressed professional, and every action (second checkout for the 5pm burst, parking, prepared foods, no very-late hours) followed from that.

## Good strategy is unexpected because focus is rare

Most organizations spread resources to placate every interest. Coherent focus is itself an advantage because no one expects anyone to actually say no.

> "Strategy is at least as much about what an organization does not do as it is about what it does."

**Cases:** Apple 1997 — Jobs cut fifteen desktop models to one, peripherals to zero, five of six retailers, inventory by 80%. Desert Storm — the envelopment was in the manuals; the surprise was actually executing such pure focus against competing service/coalition/political interests.

**Ask:** Who is disappointed by this strategy because their initiative is excluded? If we cut half our initiatives, which remaining few form a coherent punch?

---

## Insight — the second source of advantage

Advantage often comes from a viewpoint that reveals hidden asymmetry, or that redefines the unit of competition. This is usually the user's positioning, distinct from the diagnosis.

**Cases:** David and Goliath — the sling reframes size and armor as liabilities. Wal-Mart — the unit of competition became a regional network around distribution, not a single store; Kmart could not copy it one store at a time.

**Ask:** What looks like their strength but becomes weakness under a different frame? What unit are we assuming is the unit of competition, and could it be redefined?

## Four hallmarks of bad strategy

1. **Fluff** — inflated words hiding the absence of thought.
2. **Failure to face the challenge** — no obstacle named, so the strategy cannot be tested or improved.
3. **Mistaking goals for strategy** — desired results stated without a way through the difficulty.
4. **Bad strategic objectives** — a dog's dinner of many items, or a blue-sky restatement of desire.

> "If you fail to identify and analyze the obstacles, you don't have a strategy. Instead, you have either a stretch goal, a budget, or a list of things you wish would happen."

**Cases:** Chad Logan's "20/20 plan" — 20% growth and 20% margin, answered only with willpower and Jack Welch quotes; all results, no action. LAUSD — thirty-four weak schools named, then buried under seven strategies, twenty-six tactics, 234 action steps and blue-sky "transformational leadership" the plan itself admitted was unreachable.

**Tell:** if reading it produces dull annoyance, it may be bad strategy in formal clothing.

## Why bad strategy is common

It is active avoidance of hard work. Three paths: the pain of choice; template-style strategy; New-Thought belief that a positive vision substitutes for analysis and action.

**Template / charisma-in-a-can:** the fill-in-the-blanks genre — vision, mission, values, goals, strategies, initiatives — couched entirely in positive terms so it never has to diagnose. Test: could this text be pasted into another organization's plan with little change?

## Slot-misfill catalog (for auditing a draft)

- Diagnosis holding the **market opportunity** instead of the org's own challenge.
- Diagnosis holding the **customer's problem** instead of the strategist's problem (usually it is the insight/positioning).
- Guiding policy holding **segmentation or analysis** instead of an approach to the obstacle.
- Actions holding **goals** ("build X", "reach 20%").
- Actions that **conflict** rather than reinforce.

## Strategy as hypothesis (Part III)

Strategy works like a scientific hypothesis — an educated conjecture at the edge of knowledge, tested by experience, generating proprietary knowledge by probing the arena. Commit judgments to writing in advance so reality can calibrate them.

**Cases:** Schultz's Starbucks hypothesis (the Milan espresso bar in America) was revised hard by results — drop the Italian menus and opera, add chairs and takeout. Rumelt reframed the Hughes managers' demand for a guaranteed strategy as asking for a guaranteed hypothesis.

**Tools for fighting your own myopia:** make a list; use the kernel; work problem-solution; create-destroy; consult a mental panel of experts; write the diagnosis down before the meeting so you can learn from being wrong.

## Sanity checks

- Are we choosing, or placating?
- What are we not doing?
- Where does this name the hard obstacle?
- Do the actions reinforce, or compete?
- Is our advantage increasing, or static?
- What weak link caps the system?
- Did we grab the first answer, or create and destroy alternatives?
- What result would prove us wrong enough to revise?
