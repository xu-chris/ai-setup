# Framing question banks

Open this when a topic stalls and your first press isn't landing. These are wording options, not a script — pick what fits, ask one at a time, and judge every answer with the verdict table in SKILL.md.

## Unpacking vague words (VAGUE verdict)

There is no "fast", only "faster than X". Every comparative needs a reference point and an example.

- "What does that mean, concretely?"
- "Slower / harder / worse than what?"
- "Give me an example of that. Now give me an example of *not* that."
- "When did that last happen? Walk me through it."
- "You said 'they' — who exactly? Name one."

"Like" is not a cause, it's an excuse for not knowing why. "It's important to be healthy" is a wish; the cause is "I want to fit into the suit for the wedding next month." Test a self-flattering claim: "Name three other things you've done recently that show that."

## Reconstructing an episode (evidence standard)

You want a real remembered event, not a generalization. Details unlock memory.

- "Think of the last time this happened. What day was it — during the week, the weekend?"
- "Who was with you? Did you call them, or did they call you?"
- "What were you doing right before?"
- "What did you do next? And after that?"
- Slow the film down: "You walk in — or are you already there? Take me through it moment by moment."

Chase energy: when you hear emphasis, a sigh, all-caps speech, or an expletive, stop and dig — "Wait, tell me more about that. Why does that matter?" The real push is usually buried in the emotion.

Play dumb to lower the stakes (and mean it): "I'm confused — what happens at that step exactly?" The moment someone feels judged, they shut down.

## Five whys (UNSUPPORTED verdict)

Get from a stated wish to a cause the owner can act on. Run each "because" back through the verdict table — a hop with no evidence is UNSUPPORTED, not progress. Do not supply the next cause yourself.

Worked chain (note the branch and the evidence check at each hop):

- **Ask:** "Filmmakers want a bulk-upload button." (a solution — mine it) → "What goes wrong today that a bulk upload would fix?"
- **Why 1:** "They upload festival packages one file at a time and it takes forever." → *evidence?* "Show me — how many files, how long?" → "Twelve files, ~20 minutes each submission." PASS.
- **Why 2:** "Why is that a problem worth solving now?" → "They submit to many festivals in a two-week window and can't keep up." → *evidence?* named two filmmakers who did this last month. PASS.
- **Branch appears:** "Is the pain the upload time, or having to redo it per festival?" → follow both.
  - Branch A: upload speed per submission.
  - Branch B: "The same files get re-uploaded for every festival because nothing is reused." → this is a different, deeper cause.
- **Why 3 (branch B):** "Why re-upload rather than reuse?" → "There's no place that holds their past materials." → actionable, owner-controlled. **Stop here** — a cause the product owns, not a symptom, not a person.

Stop conditions: an actionable cause within the owner's control. Not a symptom ("it's slow"), not a person ("the intern forgot" → ask "why did the process allow it?"), and not mechanically at five.

## Four forces (topics 4 & 6 — why they switch, why they don't)

Progress happens only when (push + pull) > (anxiety + habit). Topics 5–6 are mostly the anxiety+habit side, which is where problems get stuck.

- **Push of the situation** (topic 4/5): "What are you struggling with? What's not happening that you want to happen? Why are you dealing with this now?"
- **Pull of the new** (topic 3): "What are you hoping for? What's different once it's solved?"
- **Anxiety of the new** (topic 6): "What worries you about changing? What could go wrong? Who else is affected?"
- **Habit of the present** (topic 4/6): "Even though it's a problem, what do you like about how it works today? What would you refuse to give up?"

The habit answers double as the "water" — what the current broken state silently provides. Removing it will startle someone; make that explicit.

## Flip technique (topic 5 — the cost)

When comparing candidates or measuring a problem's weight, flip from benefit to cost:

- Instead of "what good happens if we build this?" ask: "They can't do this today — **what are they doing instead?**" then "**What's bad about that?**"
- Start the second question with "I know this is a dumb question, but…" — it surfaces the real stakes ("a big customer almost missed a deadline").
- A workaround with real cost (missed deadlines, churn, rework) is worth shaping. "Inefficient but working" may not be.

## Multi-party enumeration (topic 2)

The solution list is a function of whose problem you decided it was — usually an unconscious decision.

- "Who else touches this? Who is upstream of them, downstream of them?"
- For each party: "What is the essence of *their* problem, in their words? What do they want, and what do they see?"
- Expect the lists to conflict. Pick the segment where the cost is sharpest; record the rest.

## Detecting SUSPECT answers

- Convenient number, no source: "Which dashboard is that in? Can you pull it up now?"
- Contradicts an earlier answer: name it. "Earlier you said the drop-off was at payment; now it's upload. Which does the data show?"
- Cake-layer test: "Name three recent cases where that happened." Vague claims can't produce three real ones.
